===============================
Despite Its Death, Destiny 2 is One of the Top Games on Steam
===============================
As once they said of the King, Destiny 2 is dead—long live Destiny 2!
+++++++++++++++++++++++++++++++

Before Destiny 2’s final update, “Moment of Triumph,” the game was averaging less than 15,000 players a day according to SteamCharts. Today, that number is closer to 90,000, after spiking to as high as 160,000. While it is implausible that this success convinces Sony to pursue Destiny 3, it nevertheless suggests that Destiny 2 might have a future attracting new players; and this supposed death might be more of a boon than it outwardly appears—even beyond the obvious Streisand Effect that the game shutting down has had.

It was Destiny 2’s death that caused me to finally understand Destiny 2. I had played it several years ago, for a couple of hours, and bounced off Destiny’s famously bad onboarding for new players. There was too much to do, and I was looking for the proper next thing to do, when in fact there wasn’t one. Destiny 2’s death caused me to approach the game in a different way: Instead of looking for the next thing I was supposed to do, I just wanted to do something, so I could see why certain people loved this game so much. I treated it more like a sandbox. And it turns out that was exactly what I was supposed to do, and I very quickly realized why Destiny’s fans believe it to be one of the greatest games ever made. Destiny 2 is an unbelievable achievement in terms of scale, narrative, and combat gameplay; it challenges generic boundaries in the gaming space by being an MMO, a competitive FPS, and a single-player campaign all merged together. It is unsurprising that somebody attempting to understand it before playing or even while playing would become discouraged.

So it was actually Destiny’s death that made it possible for me to engage with Destiny in the way it was meant to be played.

In the study of literature, we say that every text teaches the reader how to read it. Most, though, do not have a good “onboarding” process. Instead, the frictions we experience when reading indicate that we are reading in the wrong way: paying too much attention to one thing, and not another. Destiny 2 also teaches us how to read it—or play it. It accomplishes this through its numerous frictions, the very frictions that caused me as a new player to drop the game several years ago.

I tell my students something else: If something bores you, skip it; get to the end so you can see the author’s vision. Then maybe you’ll go back and see why that thing that bored you initially was important. When I initially played Destiny 2, I didn’t do that; I wanted to read in the right order, and that caused me to quit. It was only when I declared I’d skip right to the end, start playing the Final Shape campaign, and tried several exotic weapons, that I got it, and wanted, suddenly, to see everything; and what once was overwhelming became an embarrassment of gaming riches.

The reason why I initially did not like Destiny 2 some years ago was that I had refused to take my own advice.

I love Destiny 2. Now that it’s dead, maybe more people can as well.
